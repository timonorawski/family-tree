# Chusid Surname Research

## Overview
Research into the CHUSID family line, the maiden name of Shirley Chusid-Fine (b. 1932-11-12, Toronto, Canada). This is an Ashkenazi Jewish surname with direct connections to the Hasidic movement.

## Etymology and Meaning
- **Hebrew Origin**: Derived from Hebrew "chasid" (חסיד) meaning "pious one" or "devout"
- **Yiddish Form**: "khusid" - referring to a Hasid, a follower of the Hasidic movement
- **Surname Adoption**: Likely adopted during the 1804 Russian Empire statute requiring Jews to take fixed surnames
- **Significance**: The name indicates ancestors who were either followers of Hasidic Judaism or recognized for their piety

## Spelling Variations
When searching records, use ALL of these variants:
- **Primary**: Chusid, Khusid, Husid
- **Secondary**: Chusyd, Khuzid, Chasid, Hasid, Hosid, Chosed
- **Phonetic variants** (Daitch-Mokotoff Soundex): Casidoy, Casuto, Chast, Chistey, Chusit, Costa, Gaist, Gajst, Geist, Kost, Kozodoy

## Geographic Origins
### Primary Regions
- **Ukraine**: Documented Chusid family in Odessa circa 1900 (with rare mitochondrial haplogroup)
- **Poland**: Historical records from Volhynia region (16th century)

### Hasidic Movement Connection
- Movement founded in 18th century Ukraine by Rabbi Israel ben Eliezer (Baal Shem Tov)
- Spread throughout Volhynia, Podolia, Galicia, and Polish territories
- By mid-19th century, majority of Ukrainian Jews were Hasidic
- Chusid surname bearers likely descend from early adherents

## Immigration to Canada Context
### Timeline
- **1900-1927**: Great Migration period - Jewish population in Canada grew from 17,000 to 125,000
- **1927**: Restrictions began slowing immigration
- **1931**: Order-in-council PC 695 effectively halted Eastern European Jewish immigration

### Toronto Jewish Settlement
- Early 20th century: Settlement in "The Ward" district
- 1930s: Moved to Kensington Market (80% Jewish population)
- Spadina district: Hub of textile/garment industry employment
- By 1931: 45,000 Jews in Toronto, mostly Polish immigrants
